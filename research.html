Research

Extreme heat, urban equity
& the AI that maps both

My doctoral work turns toward extreme heat — predicting it with remote-sensing AI and asking who a warming city leaves behind. Below: the PhD direction taking shape at Cardiff, and the prior threads that led to it.

PhD direction · Cardiff University · from 2026 Currently shaping

Multi-scale heatwave prediction & community resilience

A heatwave is not felt evenly. My PhD asks where extreme heat will fall hardest across a warming, urbanising landscape — and who is least able to escape it. The aim is to turn a heatwave from a weather event into a map of risk, exposure and adaptation, pairing remote-sensing AI with spatial modelling and human behaviour.

Heat risk = hazard × exposure × vulnerability

A

Remote sensing × AI

Building the spatial backbone from satellite data — moving beyond hand-engineered indices toward geospatial foundation models and satellite embeddings that fuse optical, radar and climate signals into one consistent, learnable representation of the urban–regional surface.

B

Heatwave prediction

Fusing those representations with meteorological reanalysis and future-climate scenarios to predict where heatwaves strike, how intense they grow and how long they last — across space and time, not a single static snapshot.

C

Resilience & equity

Linking hazard maps back to people through agent-based modelling — simulating how elderly, low-income and outdoor-working communities actually respond to heat, so adaptation (green corridors, cooling centres) reaches those most often left behind.

Methods in focus · satellite embeddings · multi-source data fusion · spatio-temporal deep learning · agent-based modelling
